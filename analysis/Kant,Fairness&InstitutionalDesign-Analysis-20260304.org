# Kant, Fairness & Institutional Design

Human beings are not purely rational optimizers — they are fairness-seeking creatures. Charlie Munger, drawing on behavioral psychology in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, identifies what he calls the **Kantian Fairness Tendency**: the deep-seated human disposition to expect and display fairness, and to feel viscerally wronged when it is violated. This tendency, Munger argues, is underwritten by Kant's categorical imperative — the principle that one ought to "follow behavior patterns that, if followed by all others, would make the whole system work best for everybody." Together, these ideas form a powerful lens for thinking about how institutions should be designed.

## The Categorical Imperative as Institutional Logic

Kant's categorical imperative is not merely an abstract moral principle — it is a blueprint for systemic thinking. To ask "what if everyone did this?" is to force the agent out of self-interested reasoning and into the perspective of the whole. This is precisely the logic that underpins well-functioning institutions. Rules, norms, and procedures derive their legitimacy not from authority alone but from their universalizability: a rule is good if it could be adopted by all participants without the system collapsing. A tax code, a peer-review process, a research protocol — each is defensible only insofar as it survives this test.

The *PoorCharliesAlmanack.org* notes capture this concisely: the categorical imperative demands behavior that, universalized, optimizes for collective outcomes. Institutional designers who internalize this ask not "what benefits my team?" but "what policy would I accept if I did not know which role I would play?" — an echo of Rawls' veil of ignorance, itself a Kantian descendant.

## Fairness as a Stabilizing Force

Munger's observation about the abolition of slavery is instructive: "Kantian fairness Tendency was a major contributor" to one of history's most significant moral shifts. This is a claim about institutional change driven not by pure economics or raw power, but by the spread of a norm — the felt intuition that certain arrangements are simply incompatible with how people deserve to be treated.

This matters for institutional design because it suggests that **norms are load-bearing structures**. Institutions that violate the collective fairness intuition tend to erode from within, generating resistance, defection, and eventual reform. Conversely, institutions that successfully encode fairness — transparent procedures, equal standing, predictable rules — generate the trust necessary for coordination at scale. Fairness is not soft idealism; it is an engineering constraint.

## Why This Matters: Scientific Collaboration at Scale

Large scientific experiments like the CMS detector at CERN make this tension concrete. CMS involves thousands of physicists from hundreds of institutions across dozens of countries, all contributing labor, resources, and intellectual capital to a shared apparatus. No single actor controls the outcome. The system only functions if participants believe the rules are fair: that authorship credit is allocated justly, that institutional contributions are recognized proportionally, that analysis decisions are made transparently.

When these norms break down — when large institutions capture disproportionate credit, or when junior researchers' contributions are obscured — the Kantian fairness instinct activates. People defect, collaborate less openly, or leave the field. The categorical imperative reframes this: a good authorship policy is one that every contributor, regardless of seniority or institutional affiliation, could rationally endorse from behind a veil of ignorance about their own position. Designing CMS governance by this standard is not just ethically preferable — it is instrumentally necessary for the experiment to function.

Munger's behavioral framing and Kant's moral philosophy converge on the same institutional prescription: build systems you would be willing to inhabit in any role. The test of a fair institution is not whether the powerful find it acceptable, but whether the whole system, universalized, works for everybody.
